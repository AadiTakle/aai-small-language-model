SOCRATIC TUTOR ADEQUACY JUDGE & REWRITER -- TRAINING DATA SAMPLE
================================================================================
One representative example per verdict category (the 5-way taxonomy).

TASK: Given a K-12 math problem, its correct solution, the conversation so far,
and a CANDIDATE tutor message, the model judges that message and outputs
{verdict, reasoning, rewritten_message}. For messages that are not "adequate" it
also produces a safe Socratic rewrite; for "adequate" messages no rewrite is
needed (rewritten_message = null).

The model's INPUT is exactly: problem, correct_solution, conversation_history,
candidate_message. (Grade band shown for reader context only.)

Source: data/raw/v6_consensus.jsonl -- consensus-jury labeled training pool.
Examples were chosen to cleanly encapsulate each category.


================================================================================
1) ADEQUATE      [grade band: K-2  |  id: exp-k2a-005]
   The tutor message is pedagogically sound: it engages the student's specific
   question and guides with a question, revealing neither the answer nor the
   key step.
================================================================================

INPUT (what the model sees)
  Problem:           There are 5 birds on a branch. 4 more birds land on it. How many birds are there now?
  Correct solution:  5 + 4 = 9 birds.
  Conversation so far:
      Student: Do I add or subtract the birds?
  Candidate tutor message (the turn under review):
      "When more birds land, does the total number of birds go up or down?"

TARGET OUTPUT (what the model is trained to produce)
  verdict:            adequate
  reasoning:          The student asked whether to add or subtract, and the tutor's question
                      about the total going up or down (since birds are landing, per the
                      problem) guides them to addition without saying 'add' or computing 9.
  rewritten_message:  null   (already adequate -- no rewrite needed)

================================================================================
2) GIVES_FINAL_ANSWER      [grade band: 9-12  |  id: exp-912a-020]
   The tutor states the final answer outright, ending the student's thinking.
================================================================================

INPUT (what the model sees)
  Problem:           Factor x^2 + 2x - 15.
  Correct solution:  Need two numbers multiplying to -15 and adding to 2: 5 and -3. So x^2+2x-15 = (x+5)(x-3).
  Conversation so far:
      Student: I can't find the right numbers.
  Candidate tutor message (the turn under review):
      "The numbers are 5 and -3, so it factors as (x+5)(x-3)."

TARGET OUTPUT (what the model is trained to produce)
  verdict:            gives_final_answer
  reasoning:          The reply reveals both the numbers '5 and -3' and the fully factored
                      form '(x+5)(x-3)'.
  rewritten_message:  Think about pairs of numbers whose product is -15 -- could one of
                      those pairs also add up to 2?

================================================================================
3) GIVES_AWAY_KEY_STEP      [grade band: 6-8  |  id: exp-68b-046]
   The tutor hands over the pivotal step -- here it does the hard computation
   for the student, leaving only a trivial final step. Note it stops SHORT of
   stating the final answer; that is what separates it from GIVES_FINAL_ANSWER.
================================================================================

INPUT (what the model sees)
  Problem:           Find the mean of test scores: 85, 90, 75, 80, 95
  Correct solution:  Sum = 85+90+75+80+95 = 425. Divide by 5: mean = 85.
  Conversation so far:
      Student: Do I need to add them all before dividing?
  Candidate tutor message (the turn under review):
      "Yes -- add them up first: 85+90+75+80+95 = 425, then divide by how many scores there are."

TARGET OUTPUT (what the model is trained to produce)
  verdict:            gives_away_key_step
  reasoning:          The tutor computes the exact sum '425' for the student, leaving only
                      the trivial final division step.
  rewritten_message:  Yes, adding comes first. Can you add up 85, 90, 75, 80, and 95
                      yourself and see what total you get?

================================================================================
4) MISMATCHED_CALIBRATION      [grade band: 3-5  |  id: v1-35-19]
   The tutor is on-topic but pitched at the wrong level -- re-teaching
   something the student has already demonstrated they know.
================================================================================

INPUT (what the model sees)
  Problem:           What is 9 x 4?
  Correct solution:  9 x 4 = 36. One way: 10 x 4 = 40, then subtract one 4: 40 - 4 = 36.
  Conversation so far:
      Student: 9 times 4 is 36.
  Candidate tutor message (the turn under review):
      "Here's how times tables work: you can skip-count by 4s, so 4, 8, 12, and keep going until you've counted nine of them."

TARGET OUTPUT (what the model is trained to produce)
  verdict:            mismatched_calibration
  reasoning:          The student already gave the correct product, '9 times 4 is 36', yet
                      the tutor re-teaches basic skip-counting they clearly no longer need.
  rewritten_message:  That's the correct product, nice work. Can you explain the strategy
                      you used to get it, and then try 9 times 5 the same way? What do you
                      predict?

================================================================================
5) VAGUE_UNHELPFUL      [grade band: 9-12  |  id: exp-912a-049]
   The tutor offers no actionable, problem-specific guidance -- generic
   encouragement or filler.
================================================================================

INPUT (what the model sees)
  Problem:           Factor 49x^2 - 100.
  Correct solution:  Write as (7x)^2 - 10^2 = (7x-10)(7x+10).
  Conversation so far:
      Student: I don't recognize what kind of factoring this needs for 49x^2 - 100.
  Candidate tutor message (the turn under review):
      "You've got this, just believe in yourself and try your best!"

TARGET OUTPUT (what the model is trained to produce)
  verdict:            vague_unhelpful
  reasoning:          This is pure encouragement with no mention of the specific numbers
                      '49' or '100' or any factoring pattern.
  rewritten_message:  Are 49x^2 and 100 both perfect squares? What numbers, when squared,
                      give you those?
